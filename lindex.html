INTRODUCTION

The dependence on portable and electronic devices has become a defining aspect of modern daily life. Mobile phones, laptops, tablets, and other personal technologies are now essential tools for communication, academic productivity, digital transactions, and access to online platforms. In educational institutions, workplaces, transportation hubs, and commercial areas, individuals rely on these devices to accomplish time-sensitive tasks, retrieve information, and remain connected to both personal and institutional networks.

As reliance on rechargeable devices increases, users frequently encounter situations in which battery depletion interrupts productivity, communication, and mobility. In schools and universities, students often carry laptops and smartphones throughout the day for academic research, digital submissions, and virtual learning platforms. In many environments, however, charging solutions remain limited, unsecured, or inconveniently placed.

The risks associated with unsecured charging areas extend beyond the loss of physical property. Personal devices often store sensitive content such as academic files, identification details, financial applications, and private communications. The theft or tampering of a smartphone or laptop can result in compromised data, disrupted academic progress, or unauthorized access to institutional platforms.

LITERATURE REVIEW

The rapid adoption of renewable energy technologies has prompted significant research into hybrid renewable energy systems (HRES), particularly those combining solar photovoltaic (PV) panels and wind turbines, with battery storage for energy management. Hybrid systems address the inherent intermittency of individual energy sources by leveraging the complementary characteristics of solar and wind power. Sahri, Belkhier, Tamalouzt, Ullah, Shaw, and Techato (2021) presented a microgrid system integrating PV, wind, battery, fuel cells, and supercapacitors.

Control and automation through microcontrollers are central to the effective implementation of hybrid systems. Khechchab et al. (2025) designed a real-time intelligent control system using microcontrollers to manage hybrid renewable energy systems for EV charging. Their study illustrated that embedded controllers could dynamically adjust energy distribution based on real-time conditions.

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

Renewable Energy System Theory - This theory emphasizes the integration, optimization, and management of multiple renewable energy sources to achieve reliable, efficient, and sustainable power generation.

Systems Theory - Introduced by Ludwig von Bertalanffy, this theory considers any project as a collection of interrelated subsystems that function together to achieve a specific objective.

Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) - Developed by Davis (1989), this theory provides a framework for understanding how users adopt and interact with new technology.

  1. To identify the needs requirements as to hardware and software
  2. To determine the features of the project including RFID-based authentication, hybrid renewable energy, lockable individual compartments, LCD status display, and buzzer/LED indicators

METHODOLOGY (IPO Model)

INPUT: Hardware components (solar panel, wind turbine, Arduino Nano, RFID reader, solenoid lock, battery bank, LCD, buzzer, LEDs) and Software (Arduino IDE, C++ programming, flowcharts)

PROCESS: Phase I (Planning) - Identifying materials and creating circuit diagrams. Phase II (Design and Development) - Constructing and integrating solar and wind components, programming the microcontroller. Phase III (Testing) - Analyzing performance results.

OUTPUT: Wind and Solar Powered Cabinet Charging Station with Anti-Theft System

HARDWARE COMPONENTS

  • Arduino Nano - Main controller for the anti-theft mechanism
  • RDM6300 125kHz RFID Reader - Reads RFID cards for user authentication
  • 12V Solenoid Lock - Physically secures the cabinet compartment
  • 600W Solar Panel + 300W Wind Turbine - Hybrid power generation
  • 12V 25Ah Lead Acid Battery (x4) - Energy storage system
  • 24x4 LCD Display - Shows system status and user instructions
  • Buzzer and LEDs - Provides audio/visual feedback

SOFTWARE OVERVIEW

The system was programmed using Arduino IDE with C++. The program logic includes RFID card reading, cabinet state management (available/occupied), EEPROM memory storage for persistent data, and control of relay, buzzer, and LEDs based on user authentication.

USER'S MANUAL – SYSTEM OPERATIONS

Available State: Green LED ON, LCD displays "Tap your RFID card" → User taps card → Cabinet locks → Two short beeps → Red LED ON

Occupied State: Only the registered RFID card can unlock the cabinet

Unlocking: User taps same card → Cabinet unlocks → One long beep → Green LED ON

Unauthorized Access: Wrong card during occupied state → Three short beeps → LCD shows "ACCESS DENIED"

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

The system was successfully tested and demonstrated that the hybrid renewable energy system can meet the power demands of multiple devices. The RFID-based locking mechanism effectively provided security and regulated access. The LCD, buzzer, and LEDs provided clear user feedback throughout all operations.

LIMITATIONS

  • Performance depends on environmental conditions (sunlight and wind availability)
  • Limited battery storage capacity for extended periods without renewable input
  • Basic RFID security without encryption

FUTURE DIRECTIONS

  • IoT-based energy monitoring and remote management
  • Mobile app notifications for security events
  • Biometric authentication (fingerprint)
  • GPS tracking for theft recovery
  • Higher capacity battery technology

CONCLUSION

The design and development of a wind and solar powered cabinet charging station with anti-theft system was successfully completed. The project demonstrated that renewable energy sources can be effectively integrated to support small-scale public utilities, decreasing dependence on traditional electricity. The anti-theft system enhances user confidence and promotes greater acceptance of shared charging stations. The proposed system is recommended for deployment in schools, transport terminals, and public parks.

RECOMMENDATIONS

  1. Use higher-capacity batteries for longer autonomy
  2. Integrate IoT for remote monitoring and predictive maintenance
  3. Add mobile notifications for security events
  4. Deploy in high-traffic public areas
  5. Conduct long-term durability testing
